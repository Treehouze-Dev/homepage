Every company has an onboarding process. There is a laptop waiting. There is an IT ticket. There is a first-day agenda with a tour of the office, a session with HR, and a welcome lunch. By the end of week one, the new hire has access to all the right systems. On paper, onboarding is complete.

But something important has not happened. The new hire does not yet know anyone. They are not sure who to go to when they have a question that feels too small for a formal meeting. They eat lunch alone on Tuesday. By the end of month one, they are producing, they are attending standups — and they are quietly wondering whether this place is really for them.

This is the gap that almost no company is addressing.

## The two types of onboarding

There is **operational onboarding**: the process of getting a new employee set up to work. Access, tools, policies, role clarity, initial projects. Most companies do this reasonably well.

And there is **social onboarding**: the process of helping a new employee become part of the organisation. Understanding the informal culture, building trust with colleagues, feeling like they belong. Almost no company has a structured process for this.

The problem is not that companies do not care. It is that social integration is invisible. You can track whether someone completed their compliance training. You cannot easily see whether they are building the connections they need to stay.

## What the research tells us

Gallup's 2025 Global Report found that 1 in 5 European employees considers leaving in their first week. The same research found that employees with a genuine work friendship are three times more likely to stay.

A genuine work friendship does not emerge from a welcome lunch. It emerges from repeated, low-stakes interactions — a shared problem, a casual conversation, a moment of vulnerability. These things happen naturally for some people in some environments. For many new hires, especially those joining remotely or in a city where they know no one, they do not happen at all.

## The cost of doing nothing

Early social isolation is one of the strongest predictors of early attrition. A new hire who has not built meaningful connections by day 60 is significantly more likely to leave within 12 months — regardless of how good the role is, how strong the manager is, or how competitive the salary is.

The financial cost of this is real. Replacing an employee typically costs between 50% and 200% of their annual salary when you factor in recruitment, lost productivity, and institutional knowledge. But the human cost matters too. A new hire who leaves because they felt isolated is not a statistic. They are a person who made a major life decision and it did not work out.

## What structured social integration looks like

Fixing this does not require a radical overhaul of how companies work. It requires three things:

**Visibility.** HR and People teams need to be able to see social integration signals early — not when someone resigns, but in the first weeks when there is still time to act.

**Structure.** Social connection does not happen on its own at scale. It needs prompts, nudges, introductions, and events. The same way operational onboarding has a checklist, social onboarding needs a cadence.

**Intervention.** When a new hire is falling behind socially, someone needs to know — and know what to do about it. A targeted introduction, an invitation to a team lunch, a check-in from a manager. Small actions, done at the right moment, make a significant difference.
